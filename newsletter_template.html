Industry Spotlight

Breaking: Greta Gerwig to Direct Star Wars Trilogy

In a stunning move that has sent shockwaves through Hollywood, Lucasfilm announced this week that Oscar-nominated director Greta Gerwig has signed on to helm a brand new Star Wars trilogy. Following the massive success of her Barbie film and the upcoming Narnia adaptation, Gerwig becomes the first woman to direct a mainline Star Wars theatrical release.

Sources close to the production reveal that the trilogy will explore an entirely new era of the Star Wars timeline, set 400 years after the events of The Rise of Skywalker. The announcement comes alongside the news that longtime Star Wars composer John Williams will consult on the musical direction, though a primary composer has yet to be named.

Production is slated to begin in late 2027, with a projected 2029 release for the first installment. Kathleen Kennedy stated, "Greta's visionary approach to storytelling and her ability to balance spectacle with intimate character moments make her the perfect choice to usher in this new chapter."

⭐⭐⭐⭐½

Villeneuve returns with a haunting meditation on guilt and redemption set against the backdrop of a failing arctic research station. Oscar Isaac delivers a career-best performance.

The Editorial

Why We Need More "Mid-Budget" Movies Now Than Ever

This week, as I watched The Long Shadow in a nearly empty Thursday matinee, I was struck by a sobering realization: films like this are becoming endangered species. Not because they're bad—quite the opposite—but because they exist in that increasingly narrow space between indie darlings and tentpole blockbusters.

The mid-budget film, once the backbone of cinema, is dying. We're losing the $30-60 million range movies that don't need to gross a billion dollars to be considered successful. These are films with vision, star power, and production value that aren't based on existing IP or designed to launch franchises. They're just... stories.

The Long Shadow cost an estimated $45 million—a fraction of Guardian Protocol's $200 million budget—yet delivers ten times the emotional impact. It takes risks. It trusts its audience. It doesn't hold your hand or explain every metaphor. This is cinema for adults who want to be challenged, not just entertained.

But here's the problem: these films struggle to find audiences in our current theatrical landscape. Squeezed between the MCU machine and limited-release art films, they often get lost. Streaming platforms were supposed to be their savior, but algorithmic recommendations favor the familiar over the daring.

We, as audiences, have to actively seek out and support these films. Buy tickets. Spread the word. Because if we don't, we'll wake up in a world where every movie is either a $300 million superhero spectacle or a $3 million chamber piece, with nothing in between.

— The CinePrism Editorial Team

Scene Breakdown: The Mirror Scene

From: Neon Nights (2026)

The Setup: Kira (Rinko Kikuchi) is cornered in a nightclub with an experimental holographic mirror installation. A 4-minute, seemingly one-take fight sequence that took 17 days to shoot.

The Technique

  • Practical Effects: 90% real mirrors, not CGI.
  • Choreography: Designed by 87Eleven (John Wick team) accounting for spatial disorientation.
  • Sound Design: Absence of music makes every glass crack visceral.

Why It Works: The mirrors serve as metaphor: in a world of false realities, the fight becomes about finding truth through reflections. Watch for the mirror shatter at 2:43 – slow-motion fragments falling while the fight continues in real-time.

Cinephile Trivia

What is a "Dutch Angle"?

Also known as a "Dutch tilt" or "canted angle," this technique involves tilting the camera on its roll axis so the horizon line is diagonal. It doesn't come from the Netherlands—it's a mistranslation of "Deutsch" (German), referring to German Expressionist cinema of the 1920s.

When to Use It:

  • To create psychological unease or disorientation.
  • To suggest danger or tension.
  • To visually represent a disturbed mental state.

Homework: Watch the opening of The Long Shadow and count the Dutch angles (Hint: Zero. Villeneuve favors symmetry).
